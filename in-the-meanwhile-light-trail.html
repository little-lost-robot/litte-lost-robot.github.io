In the Meanwhile...

In January & February 2022, In The Meanwhile presents a trail of artworks lighting up empty shop windows around Bath.

All artworks featured are by Bath based artists. The project has been funded by Bristol + Bath Creative R + D and Arts Council England.

In The Meanwhile, is a project organised by Little Lost Robot Studio and supported by Bath Spa University and The Studio, working with B&NES Council, to bring temporarily redundant shop spaces into productive use. The Covid-19 pandemic and subsequent economic crisis has seen a large number of shops in the city centre become empty; these vacated spaces are highly visible. In The Meanwhile, seeks to support the council to reinvigorate these spaces during this interim period.

“Rondo Starlight i & ii”

Inspired by plaster ceiling roses, these works are made from porcelain with leds. Working with a variety of clays and at different scales every piece is unique as the work continually evolves. For example the use of stains wedged into clay. The Rondo series incorporated LEDs within wall hung pieces enabled by a collaboration with a fabricator. Construction techniques used include throwing on a potters wheel and handbuilding with white and coloured porcelain.

Jo Taylor

Jo Taylor works in clay, using drawing & print to support the making process. Her dynamic pieces respond to architectural elements such as ornamental plaster ceilings, Corinthian Columns and wrought iron. Aiming to express visual drama and work with light and shadow she combines techniques such as the potter’s wheel and hand building to create unique contemporary pieces that combine the historic & contemporary and the functional, sculptural & decorative.

“Urban Rookery”

A visual experience made with six 6 huge rooks nests that were rescued when a group of trees was being felled. The careful construction of temporarily used nests and temporality of ‘in the meanwhile’ spaces versus comfort and security links with ideas of displacement and the emptiness of now functionless nests and empty shop windows. Rooks are communal breeders so placing these nests in a community setting in a city centre highlights our complex relationship with the natural world.

Juliet Duckworth

“My work shows concern with temporality and topography of the earth’s surface. My practice deals with materiality, scale and physical process. Artworks are constructed using natural materials and found objects which when transformed into sculptural forms are often ephemeral. The liminal space just above and just below the ground inspires me. The ordinary and often unnoticed surfaces under our feet are a continuing source of material.”

“Dancing Forest”

Set of three mixed media sculptures inspired by the movement of Lotus - a plant revered for centuries as a symbol of peacefulness and growth, and metronomes - devices used by musicians to mark and keep time while creating new melodies. Numerous LEDs are housed in each head, controlled by an arduino using sine waves to mimic Spring light.

Kate McGregor Michael - Artist in Residence

Trained as a ceramicist, sculptor and educationalist. I have always felt humbled by nature and believe that this feeling resonates amongst us on personal, local and national and global levels. I aim to develop the narrative in my work around the promise of new life, rebirth and growth contained within natural forms; seedpods and seedheads. I want to represent our deeper connection with nature and its potential to restore when we allow ourselves to be present in those spaces.

“The Times of COVID”

This stained glass window was constructed during the first UK lockdown in March 2020. It depicts the events of the Coronavirus pandemic as they unfolded. This artwork addresses concepts of truth, fake news, sobriety, satire, and the values of our society all in the context of contemporary craftsmanship.

Lauren Cattle

This work highlights the values and belief systems of contemporary society, by drawing upon symbols of common culture, world news, commerce, and capitalism. Her work references the insignificant, disastrous and delightful aspects of life, placing them in conjunction, to explore how context changes meaning. These comparisons of travesty and triviality do not intend to judge, instead, they aim to question the prevalence of social construction and re-frame our perception of truth. Laurens work is layered with juxtaposition and satire, it jumps from sobriety to silliness to mobilise challenging topics and jolt its audience from the familiar to the unexpected.

“Turtle”

A turtle constructed with recycled and scrap materials. An original commission by Little Lost Robot Studio for The Lanterns at Chester Zoo, produced by Wild Rumpus in December 2021. The turtle featured on an episode of the BBC’s Blue Peter programme which was filmed at Chester Zoo and aired on 25th November 2021. The turtle was designed and created by Ruby Sant with fabrication by Juliet Webb and Tom Cherry and coding by Dave Webb.

“Flowers”

These biologically inspired, flower sculptures. This oasis aims to passively remind us all to take it slowly, breathe, feel safe and allow playfulness. Not replacing nature, but representing our relationship with the environment and exploring possibilities of technological innovation to aid ecological sustainability. Designed and created by Ruby Sant with fabrication by Tom Cherry and coding by Dave Webb.

Little Lost Robot Studio

“We make immersive, digital and installation artwork about minority groups wellness & inclusivity. We present a vision of the future that is gloriously humane in all its messy domestic parts. Our aim is to demystify emerging technologies. We encourage and support the exploration of real world applications for technologies that will improve the daily lives of real people and places. Putting health and well-being at the core of the design process. Co-working with local people to bring visibility to the pressures on contemporary standards of living.”

“Where The Wild Things Are”

A Bosch style collection of ‘stained glass’ wild monsters created by Key Stage One pupils at Paulton Infant School. The children worked really hard and used their imaginations to express their inner monsters. They made their designs on paper and then traced them onto acrylic and decorated their monsters with scrap acetate and tissue. The monsters were then added to the scenery to make a menagerie of many heads, eyes, legs, tails and teeth!

Paulton Infant School

Paulton Infant School in B&NES kindly let Little Lost Robot Studio take over Key Stage One for a series of creative workshops based in the school, in November 2021. This is one of the youngest sets of workshop participants Little Lost Robot has had the pleasure of teaching. We were blown away by the children's enthusiasm and engagement with the themes and the imagination and energy they put into their creations.

“Table Named The Earth”

Table Named The Earth is a sculpture presenting a tabletop that is painted in colours portraying the natural pigments we come across in nature as floating shapes and forms. Its metal frame goes wild around the glass, presenting us with the image of fake freedom and movement. The work is about domesticated Earth and confusion.

Ugne Spudyte

My work is a reflection of writing, poems, observations in nature, human behaviour and dreams. A mixture of abstract and surreal approaches to materials and texture; I mostly work with paint and stained glass. Sculpture, photography and drawing are keen elements to my practice. I am a spontaneous maker; I allow the art to dictate the next step; lead itself to the final line. My way of making is experimental from beginning to end, which isn’t systematic, but open to speculations.
